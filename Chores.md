# The Complete Chores By Age Guide (5-18) - UK English Authority Hub

For **ages 5–14**, the three **Core tasks** use the same **DooCoins reward values** as each `chores-for-{age}-year-olds.html` page (and `chores-by-age-combined-reference.md`). Wording here follows **UK household English**: for example we say **toy box or storage boxes**, not toy “bins” (in British English a **bin** is usually rubbish), and **washing basket** or **laundry basket**, not a laundry “hamper” (in British English a **hamper** is often food or gifts). Anything under **Extra UK ideas** is add-on editorial content only.

## Index Page Overview: The Balance of Household Help

Expert parenting advice suggests a "Commission vs. Contribution" model. Daily habits like making one's bed are "Contributions" (unpaid), while extra tasks like washing the car are "Commissions" (earning DooCoins).

## Age 5

### Summary

A 5-year-old is developmentally ready for putting toys away in the toy box or storage boxes, placing dirty clothes in the washing basket, and setting napkins on the table. These short tasks build consistency, attention, and family contribution skills when repeated daily with simple rewards.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

- Put toys away into the toy box or storage boxes (3 coins)
- Put dirty clothes in the washing basket (3 coins)
- Set napkins at dinner spots (4 coins)

**Extra UK ideas**

- Pair up clean socks (3 coins)
- Dust low skirting boards or coffee tables (3 coins)

### Tip

Use picture checklists and keep requests one step at a time. This reduces overload and increases completion consistency.

### FAQ

- **Q:** Should 5-year-olds have chores?
  **A:** Yes. Simple chores support responsibility and confidence at this age.
- **Q:** How long should chores take at age 5?
  **A:** Usually 2 to 8 minutes per task works best.

## Age 6

### Summary

A 6-year-old is developmentally ready for making their bed, feeding a pet, and clearing their plate. Predictable routines and small rewards help children this age follow through with less parent prompting.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

- Make bed each morning (4 coins)
- Feed pet with supervision (5 coins)
- Clear own plate after meals (4 coins)

**Extra UK ideas**

- Empty small wastepaper bins into the main rubbish bin (4 coins)
- Help carry light groceries from the car (4 coins)

### Tip

Tie chores to anchors like breakfast and dinner so routines feel automatic instead of negotiable.

### FAQ

- **Q:** What chores can a 6-year-old do?
  **A:** Most can handle simple room and table tasks with supervision.
- **Q:** Should chores be daily at age 6?
  **A:** Yes, daily repetition usually builds habits faster.

A 7-year-old is developmentally ready for resetting their room, sorting dishwasher utensils, and packing their backpack for the next day. At this age, clear expectations and visible checklists improve completion rates and reduce parent nagging.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

- Reset bedroom each evening (5 coins)
- Sort safe utensils from dishwasher (6 coins)
- Pack backpack for tomorrow (5 coins)

**Extra UK ideas**

- Help pull easy weeds in the garden (5 coins)

### FAQ

- **Q:** How do I reduce nagging at age 7?
  **A:** Use visual cues and predictable routine windows.
- **Q:** Should chores be mandatory?
  **A:** Core family contribution chores usually should be consistent and expected.

An 8-year-old is developmentally ready for loading the dishwasher, folding towels, and wiping kitchen counters. At this stage, kids can complete multi-step chores when completion standards are clear.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

## Age 9

### Summary

A 9-year-old is developmentally ready for hoovering one room, sorting laundry by colour, and packing school lunch items. Children at this age benefit from clearer ownership and measurable expectations.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

- Hoover bedroom or hallway (8 coins)
- Sort lights and darks for laundry (7 coins)
- Pack lunch basics for school (7 coins)

**Extra UK ideas**

- Clean out the inside of the car (pick up rubbish) (8 coins)
- Put away own clean folded clothes (7 coins)

### Tip

Shift from reminders to accountability check-ins. Ask "is it complete?" instead of re-instructing every step.

### FAQ

- **Q:** How often should chores be checked?
  **A:** Once daily plus a weekly routine review is usually enough.
- **Q:** How do I avoid power struggles?
  **A:** Set the rules in advance and keep them consistent.

## Age 10

### Summary

A 10-year-old is developmentally ready for unloading the dishwasher, changing bed sheets, and taking out recycling. Age 10 is a strong time to shift from reminders toward routine ownership.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

- Unload dishwasher safely (9 coins)
- Change and tidy bed sheets (9 coins)
- Take out recycling weekly (8 coins)

**Extra UK ideas**

- Hand-wash large pots and pans (9 coins)

### FAQ

- **Q:** Should rewards increase with age?

- **Q:** Can a 10-year-old manage weekly chores?
  **A:** Yes, most can with one schedule review each week.

## Age 11

An 11-year-old is developmentally ready for prepping lunch ingredients, transferring laundry, and cleaning shared surfaces. At this age, giving more ownership over timing often improves follow-through.

### Chores

**Core tasks**

- Prep sandwich and fruit for lunch (10 coins)
- Transfer laundry to dryer (9 coins)

## Age 15-16

### Summary

Mid-teens are ready for significant household responsibility, including full laundry management and complex meal prep. Chores at this age should encourage long-term accountability and independence.

### Chores

- Empty the dishwasher (14 coins)
- Manage own laundry (wash, dry, and fold) (16 coins)
- Cook two full family dinners per week (20 coins)
- Hoover and deep-clean shared living spaces (18 coins)
- Intensive garden work (hedges or weeding) (20 coins)

### Tip

Focus on life skills rather than just "getting tasks done". Discuss the logic behind household management, like budgeting for meals or seasonal garden care.

### FAQ

- **Q:** How do I keep a 16-year-old motivated?
  **A:** Link rewards to larger privileges or financial goals that support their growing independence.
- **Q:** Should teens do chores during exam periods?
  **A:** Maintain a base level of responsibility but offer flexibility to ensure they can manage their study schedule.

## Age 17-18

### Summary

Young adults should focus on chores that build self-sufficiency for independent living, such as grocery shopping and basic home maintenance.

### Chores

- Empty the dishwasher (15 coins)
- Full grocery shop within a set budget (25 coins)
- Plan, shop for, and cook family meals (25 coins)
- Perform minor home repairs or furniture assembly (20 coins)
- Manage personal budget and household bill tracking (20 coins)

### Tip

Shift the relationship to a "partnership" model. Involve them in higher-level household decisions to prepare them for their own future home.

### FAQ

- **Q:** Is it too late to start a chore routine at 17?
  **A:** No. Frame it as "adulting practice" essential for moving out or going to university.
- **Q:** How do rewards work for young adults?
  **A:** Focus on high-value rewards or "commissions" that help them save for their own independent goals.
